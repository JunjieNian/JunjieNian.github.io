CoT Folding: Structural Analysis of LLM Reasoning Trajectories

Treating Chain-of-Thought reasoning as analogous to protein folding. The framework measures pairwise similarity between token segments using neuron activation patterns (NAD) to reveal how reasoning "folds" in activation space, forming compact cores, long-range return connections, and drifting branches. Proposes Native Fold Score (NFS), a fully unsupervised, parameter-free quality metric for CoT reasoning that requires no correctness labels.

Multilingual Neuron Analysis for LLM Reasoning

Building on the NAD (Neuron Agreement Decoding) framework, this project extends neuron-level analysis to multilingual settings. Analyzes 2,250 math reasoning samples across 18 languages from the Polymath-Top dataset to investigate cross-language neural activation patterns. Key findings include: quality reasoning neurons are 99.8% language-agnostic across all 18 languages; moderate language mixing (middle Shannon entropy) outperforms both monolingual and highly mixed reasoning by +68.8%; and code-switching exhibits an inverted-U relationship with accuracy.

80/20 Rule of Token Entropy in LLM Reasoning

Independent reproduction and extension of the Qwen team's work Beyond the 80/20 Rule (project page, NeurIPS 2025), which discovers that token entropy in LLM reasoning chains follows an 80/20 distribution -- only ~20% of tokens carry high entropy and drive effective reasoning, while the remaining ~80% are low-entropy structural tokens. Extends the original analysis with five-metric comparison (Entropy, Confidence, Gini, LogProb, SelfCert), bimodal word analysis, multilingual CoT analysis (Chinese, Japanese, Korean), and entropy-frequency correlation study across 539K+ samples and 70M+ token instances.

CubeClosure: Mathematical Proof & 3D Visualization of the Cube Movie Mechanism

A mathematical reconstruction of the closed room-moving mechanism from the 1997 sci-fi film Cube. Proves that collision-free room rearrangement is achievable via a 26-axis permutation codebook, where each room is assigned a three-digit coordinate on all 26 movement axes (6 face + 12 edge + 8 vertex directions). Includes a backtracking solver for codebook generation and an interactive Three.js-based 3D visualization that lets users step through room movements phase by phase, inspect encodings, and explore different grid sizes (3, 5, 6, 8).
